Gender Pay Gap in India: A Reality and the Way Forward

An Empirical Approach Using Quantile Regression Technique

Abstract

There is a growing body of empirical work on gender studies that mostly focus on the gender-wise representation of individuals in different sectors. One such study is the wage gap attributed to gender. In this article, we have tried to focus on a detailed study of the gender wage gap in the Indian context. The study was carried out on the most recent Employment-Unemployment Survey carried out by NSS for the year 2012. The study was based on the personal characteristics as well as the characteristics of the job undertaken by the employees. Ordinary least square regression and linear quartile regression model were used for analysis. In our study we have come up with a few interesting determinants of wage inequality based mostly on the personal traits. For women, personal characteristic like age was highly significant determinant of wage whereas in case of men more industry specific determinants were significant.

Literature Review

The gender pay gap in India remains a significant issue, despite progress in various sectors. Studies consistently reveal disparities in earnings between men and women, attributed to factors like age, education, and career breaks.





The gender wage pay gap by race reflects disparities in earnings based on both gender and racial backgrounds. It highlights that women from certain racial or ethnic groups earn less than men from the same group, and often even less compared to men from other racial or ethnic backgrounds. This complex issue is influenced by historical inequalities, systemic biases, and social factors that intersect to create varying levels of wage gaps for different groups of women.





The gender pay gap in India is marked by a significant difference in earnings between men and women. On average, women earn around 20-30% less than men. This gap is influenced by factors such as occupational segregation, education levels, limited representation in leadership roles, unpaid care work, and wage discrimination. Despite efforts, women's underrepresentation in higher-paying sectors and unequal treatment in the workforce contribute to this persistent disparity.









Indian women earn 19% less than men due to a combination of factors including occupational segregation, limited access to higher-paying roles, societal norms, and wage discrimination. These elements contribute to an unequal distribution of opportunities and resources in the workforce, leading to the observed gender pay gap.









Conclusion

The study confirms the persistence of the gender wage gap, with personal characteristics like age, education, and marital status contributing to the disparity. Reliance on older survey data and lack of contextual understanding limit the study's depth. It suggests that societal roles and career breaks hold back women's progress. Lack of transparency in salary information and hesitancy during negotiations exacerbate the issue. Bridging the gap requires transparency in pay, proactive negotiation, and encouragement of STEM education. Accenture proposes digital skills, career planning, and tech immersion to close the gap by 2030. Government intervention in minimum wage regulations and mandatory pay gap disclosure, inspired by initiatives like Theresa May's, could drive change.
